  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Why This Chapter Matters</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Fielding and captaincy are the hidden grammar of cricket. They show how a team creates pressure without the ball being hit.</p>
    <p>This chapter gives you the background that childhood sports followers usually collect slowly through repeated matches, arguments, highlights, and newspaper reports. Read it as a foundation, then attach the ideas to real sports news using the source links below.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Core Material</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Field placement tells you the plan. Slips mean catching edges. Deep fielders protect boundaries. Close catchers pressure batters against spin. A captain changes field according to batter, bowler, pitch, and match situation.</p>
    <p>Wicket-keeping is not only catching. It includes footwork, standing up to spin, stumpings, DRS advice, energy, and reading the batter. MS Dhoni is a major reference for calm keeping and decision-making under pressure.</p>
    <p>Captaincy involves bowling changes, field changes, match-ups, review decisions, and tempo control. A captain can win small moments before the scoreboard shows it.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Vocabulary Bank</h3>
  <section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
    <ul>
      <li><strong>slip</strong>: catching fielder behind the batter on off side</li>
      <li><strong>stumping</strong>: keeper dismisses batter outside crease</li>
      <li><strong>run-out</strong>: fielder breaks wicket while batter is short</li>
      <li><strong>DRS</strong>: decision review system</li>
      <li><strong>field setting</strong>: placement of fielders</li>
      <li><strong>bowling change</strong>: captain replacing one bowler with another</li>
    </ul>
  </section>

  <h2 class="act-section-heading"><span>50</span>Weekly Conversation Drills</h2>
  <p class="act-essence-box">Sports confidence grows through speaking. Weekly drills turn reading into natural conversation.</p>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Why This Chapter Matters</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Sports confidence grows through speaking. Weekly drills turn reading into natural conversation.</p>
    <p>This chapter gives you the background that childhood sports followers usually collect slowly through repeated matches, arguments, highlights, and newspaper reports. Read it as a foundation, then attach the ideas to real sports news using the source links below.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Core Material</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Drill one: explain one result in thirty seconds. Include sport, teams or players, score, turning point, and consequence.</p>
    <p>Drill two: describe one player by role, not fame. Drill three: make one prediction and give a reason. Drill four: ask one intelligent question when you do not know.</p>
    <p>Repeat with different sports. Cricket teaches scorecards, football teaches tables, tennis teaches pressure points, and Olympic sports teach qualification and ranking.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Worked Example</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Suppose a headline uses this topic but gives very little background. Do not begin by arguing. First identify the event and competition. Second identify the role of the main player, team, number, or condition. Third explain the consequence in one calm sentence. In this chapter, the first words to watch are summary, prediction, follow-up question.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Common Mistakes</h3>
  <section class="investment-box investment-box-event">
    <ul>
      <li>Reading only the headline and missing the match context.</li>
      <li>Using a term in conversation before connecting it to a real score, table, player, or event.</li>
      <li>Treating one clip, one statistic, or one fan opinion as the full story.</li>
    </ul>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Vocabulary Bank</h3>
  <section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
    <ul>
      <li><strong>summary</strong>: short explanation</li>
      <li><strong>prediction</strong>: reasoned expectation</li>
      <li><strong>follow-up question</strong>: question that deepens conversation</li>
      <li><strong>role description</strong>: explaining a player&#x27;s job</li>
      <li><strong>turning point</strong>: moment that changed match</li>
      <li><strong>consequence</strong>: what changes after result</li>
    </ul>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Study Path</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Read this chapter once for meaning, then open one source below and find a real example of the topic. Add three notes to your sports notebook: one vocabulary word, one sentence of context, and one question for later.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Practice</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Record a one-minute voice note summarizing a match. Listen once and improve the structure.</p>
  </section>

  <section class="investment-box investment-box-theory">

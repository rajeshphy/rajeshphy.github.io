  <h2 class="act-section-heading"><span>47</span>Debate Templates and Opinions</h2>
  <p class="act-essence-box">You can join sports debates without pretending to know everything. Use a structured opinion: claim, reason, evidence, limitation, and prediction.</p>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Why This Chapter Matters</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>You can join sports debates without pretending to know everything. Use a structured opinion: claim, reason, evidence, limitation, and prediction.</p>
    <p>This chapter gives you the background that childhood sports followers usually collect slowly through repeated matches, arguments, highlights, and newspaper reports. Read it as a foundation, then attach the ideas to real sports news using the source links below.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Core Material</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Template one: &#x27;I rate this player because [role] plus [evidence], but the concern is [limitation].&#x27; Template two: &#x27;This result matters because [table/ranking/tournament consequence].&#x27;</p>
    <p>A strong opinion admits uncertainty. Example: &#x27;India looked better in the middle overs, but I want to see them against stronger pace before judging.&#x27;</p>
    <p>Avoid empty extremes like &#x27;best ever&#x27; or &#x27;useless&#x27; unless you can support them. Sports debate is sharper when you compare roles and contexts.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Worked Example</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Suppose a headline uses this topic but gives very little background. Do not begin by arguing. First identify the event and competition. Second identify the role of the main player, team, number, or condition. Third explain the consequence in one calm sentence. In this chapter, the first words to watch are claim, evidence, limitation.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Common Mistakes</h3>
  <section class="investment-box investment-box-event">
    <ul>
      <li>Reading only the headline and missing the match context.</li>
      <li>Using a term in conversation before connecting it to a real score, table, player, or event.</li>
      <li>Treating one clip, one statistic, or one fan opinion as the full story.</li>
    </ul>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Vocabulary Bank</h3>
  <section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
    <ul>
      <li><strong>claim</strong>: main opinion</li>
      <li><strong>evidence</strong>: reason or fact supporting opinion</li>
      <li><strong>limitation</strong>: what the opinion does not prove</li>
      <li><strong>prediction</strong>: what you expect next</li>
      <li><strong>comparison</strong>: judging two players or teams by same frame</li>
      <li><strong>bias</strong>: preference that affects judgment</li>
    </ul>
  </section>

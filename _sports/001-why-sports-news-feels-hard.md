  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Why This Chapter Matters</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Sports news feels difficult because every sentence assumes background memory: tournament format, player role, rivalry, recent form, and a few technical words. Once those layers are separated, the news becomes readable.</p>
    <p>This chapter gives you the background that childhood sports followers usually collect slowly through repeated matches, arguments, highlights, and newspaper reports. Read it as a foundation, then attach the ideas to real sports news using the source links below.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Core Material</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Read sports news in four layers. The first layer is the event: who played, where, and in which competition. The second layer is the result: who won, by what margin, and whether the match was close. The third layer is the consequence: table position, qualification, ranking, selection pressure, or public debate. The fourth layer is the story: rivalry, comeback, injury, milestone, or failure under pressure.</p>
    <p>A beginner usually tries to understand everything at once and gets lost. Instead, mark unknown words and move on. After ten articles, the same words will repeat: fixture, form, seed, spell, chase, clean sheet, transfer, playoff, qualifier, and ranking.</p>
    <p>The goal is not to become encyclopedic. The goal is to ask intelligent questions: What format is this? What was at stake? Which player role mattered? What changes after this result?</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Worked Example</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Suppose a headline uses this topic but gives very little background. Do not begin by arguing. First identify the event and competition. Second identify the role of the main player, team, number, or condition. Third explain the consequence in one calm sentence. In this chapter, the first words to watch are context, stake, rivalry.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Common Mistakes</h3>
  <section class="investment-box investment-box-event">
    <ul>
      <li>Reading only the headline and missing the match context.</li>
      <li>Using a term in conversation before connecting it to a real score, table, player, or event.</li>
      <li>Treating one clip, one statistic, or one fan opinion as the full story.</li>
    </ul>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Vocabulary Bank</h3>
  <section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
    <ul>
      <li><strong>context</strong>: the background that makes the result meaningful</li>
      <li><strong>stake</strong>: what a team or player can gain or lose</li>
      <li><strong>rivalry</strong>: a repeated contest with emotional or historical weight</li>
      <li><strong>form</strong>: recent performance trend</li>
      <li><strong>fixture</strong>: a scheduled match</li>
      <li><strong>turning point</strong>: the moment that changed control of the match</li>
    </ul>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Study Path</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Read this chapter once for meaning, then open one source below and find a real example of the topic. Add three notes to your sports notebook: one vocabulary word, one sentence of context, and one question for later.</p>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Practice</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Take one sports article and underline only names, tournament, result, and consequence. Ignore opinions until those four parts are clear.</p>

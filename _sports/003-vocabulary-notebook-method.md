  <h2 class="act-section-heading"><span>3</span>Vocabulary Notebook Method</h2>
  <p class="act-essence-box">Sports vocabulary sticks when every word is tied to a real match, player, or sentence. A notebook turns passive reading into active memory.</p>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Why This Chapter Matters</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Sports vocabulary sticks when every word is tied to a real match, player, or sentence. A notebook turns passive reading into active memory.</p>
    <p>This chapter gives you the background that childhood sports followers usually collect slowly through repeated matches, arguments, highlights, and newspaper reports. Read it as a foundation, then attach the ideas to real sports news using the source links below.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Core Material</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Make four columns: term, meaning, example sentence, and sport. Example: &#x27;clean sheet&#x27; means a football team conceded no goal. Sentence: &#x27;India kept a clean sheet because the goalkeeper and centre-backs controlled the box.&#x27;</p>
    <p>Write words in families. In cricket, line, length, swing, seam, spell, economy, and death overs belong together. In football, press, block, counter, transition, overlap, and clean sheet belong together.</p>
    <p>Revise by speaking. Say the sentence aloud. Sports confidence comes from using words naturally, not from silently memorizing definitions.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Worked Example</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Suppose a headline uses this topic but gives very little background. Do not begin by arguing. First identify the event and competition. Second identify the role of the main player, team, number, or condition. Third explain the consequence in one calm sentence. In this chapter, the first words to watch are term family, example sentence, phase.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Common Mistakes</h3>
  <section class="investment-box investment-box-event">
    <ul>
      <li>Reading only the headline and missing the match context.</li>
      <li>Using a term in conversation before connecting it to a real score, table, player, or event.</li>
      <li>Treating one clip, one statistic, or one fan opinion as the full story.</li>
    </ul>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Vocabulary Bank</h3>
  <section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
    <ul>
      <li><strong>term family</strong>: a cluster of related words</li>
      <li><strong>example sentence</strong>: a realistic sentence using the word</li>
      <li><strong>phase</strong>: part of a match with a specific pattern</li>
      <li><strong>role word</strong>: word that explains a player&#x27;s job</li>
      <li><strong>stat word</strong>: word that explains performance numerically</li>
      <li><strong>context word</strong>: word that explains conditions or stakes</li>
    </ul>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Study Path</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Read this chapter once for meaning, then open one source below and find a real example of the topic. Add three notes to your sports notebook: one vocabulary word, one sentence of context, and one question for later.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Practice</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Add ten words this week. For each word, write one sentence about India, Jharkhand, or a player you know.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Chapter Takeaway</h3>
  <section class="investment-box investment-box-theory">
    <p>The chapter is complete when you can explain the topic in your own words and connect it to one real match, athlete, table, ranking, or news report.</p>

  <h2 class="act-section-heading"><span>2</span>Daily Catch-Up System</h2>
  <p class="act-essence-box">A daily system prevents sports news from becoming an ocean. You only need a small repeatable circuit: headlines, one report, one table or ranking, and one highlight.</p>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Why This Chapter Matters</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>A daily system prevents sports news from becoming an ocean. You only need a small repeatable circuit: headlines, one report, one table or ranking, and one highlight.</p>
    <p>This chapter gives you the background that childhood sports followers usually collect slowly through repeated matches, arguments, highlights, and newspaper reports. Read it as a foundation, then attach the ideas to real sports news using the source links below.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Core Material</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Use the 15-minute circuit. Spend five minutes scanning headlines, five minutes reading one full report, and five minutes checking the table, scorecard, or ranking connected to that report.</p>
    <p>Do not start with social media clips. Clips show moments without structure. Start with the result and table, then watch highlights. This order teaches you what the clip actually means.</p>
    <p>Keep one primary sport and two secondary sports. For a Jharkhand learner in India, cricket can be the primary sport; football and one Olympic sport such as archery, hockey, badminton, or athletics can be secondary.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Worked Example</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Suppose a headline uses this topic but gives very little background. Do not begin by arguing. First identify the event and competition. Second identify the role of the main player, team, number, or condition. Third explain the consequence in one calm sentence. In this chapter, the first words to watch are headline scan, match report, table.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Common Mistakes</h3>
  <section class="investment-box investment-box-event">
    <ul>
      <li>Reading only the headline and missing the match context.</li>
      <li>Using a term in conversation before connecting it to a real score, table, player, or event.</li>
      <li>Treating one clip, one statistic, or one fan opinion as the full story.</li>
    </ul>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Vocabulary Bank</h3>
  <section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
    <ul>
      <li><strong>headline scan</strong>: quick reading of many titles without deep reading</li>
      <li><strong>match report</strong>: article written after a match</li>
      <li><strong>table</strong>: ranking of teams in a league or group</li>
      <li><strong>highlight</strong>: short video of important moments</li>
      <li><strong>recap</strong>: summary after an event</li>
      <li><strong>follow-up</strong>: next story after the main result</li>
    </ul>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Study Path</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Read this chapter once for meaning, then open one source below and find a real example of the topic. Add three notes to your sports notebook: one vocabulary word, one sentence of context, and one question for later.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Practice</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>For seven days, write three lines daily: biggest result, one new term, and one question you still have.</p>
  </section>

  <section class="investment-box investment-box-theory">
    <p>The chapter is complete when you can explain the topic in your own words and connect it to one real match, athlete, table, ranking, or news report.</p>

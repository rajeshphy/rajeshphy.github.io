  <h2 class="act-section-heading"><span>18</span>Batting Vocabulary</h2>
  <p class="act-essence-box">Batting language explains role, scoring speed, shot choice, and pressure inside an innings.</p>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Why This Chapter Matters</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Batting language explains role, scoring speed, shot choice, and pressure inside an innings.</p>
    <p>This chapter gives you the background that childhood sports followers usually collect slowly through repeated matches, arguments, highlights, and newspaper reports. Read it as a foundation, then attach the ideas to real sports news using the source links below.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Core Material</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>An opener faces the new ball. An anchor stabilizes an innings. A finisher handles the last overs of a chase. A power hitter clears boundaries. A batter&#x27;s role decides whether a strike rate is excellent or careless.</p>
    <p>Shot vocabulary matters: drive, cut, pull, hook, sweep, reverse sweep, loft, and ramp describe how the batter scores. Situation vocabulary matters too: powerplay, middle overs, death overs, chase, target, and required rate.</p>
    <p>Judge batting by role. A 35 off 25 can be useful in a chase; a 35 off 60 may be poor in T20 but useful on a difficult Test pitch.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Worked Example</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Suppose a headline uses this topic but gives very little background. Do not begin by arguing. First identify the event and competition. Second identify the role of the main player, team, number, or condition. Third explain the consequence in one calm sentence. In this chapter, the first words to watch are strike rate, average, boundary.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Vocabulary Bank</h3>
  <section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
    <ul>
      <li><strong>strike rate</strong>: runs per 100 balls</li>
      <li><strong>average</strong>: runs per dismissal</li>
      <li><strong>boundary</strong>: four or six</li>
      <li><strong>partnership</strong>: runs added by two batters together</li>
      <li><strong>required rate</strong>: runs per over needed in a chase</li>
      <li><strong>collapse</strong>: quick loss of wickets</li>
    </ul>
  </section>

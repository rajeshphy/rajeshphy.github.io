  <h2 class="act-section-heading"><span>34</span>Kabaddi and Contact Sports</h2>
  <p class="act-essence-box">Kabaddi is a contact sport of timing, breath, raid strategy, defensive traps, and scoreboard pressure.</p>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Why This Chapter Matters</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>Kabaddi is a contact sport of timing, breath, raid strategy, defensive traps, and scoreboard pressure.</p>
    <p>This chapter gives you the background that childhood sports followers usually collect slowly through repeated matches, arguments, highlights, and newspaper reports. Read it as a foundation, then attach the ideas to real sports news using the source links below.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Core Material</h3>
  <section class="sports-topic">
    <p>A raider enters the opponent half to score by touching defenders and returning safely. Defenders score by stopping the raider. The game swings quickly because a successful raid can remove multiple defenders.</p>
    <p>Do-or-die raids create pressure because repeated empty raids are not allowed. Super tackles reward a reduced defense. All-out gives a team extra points after all opponents are eliminated.</p>
    <p>Kabaddi reports often use momentum language: raid success, tackle efficiency, bonus point, all-out, and revival.</p>
  </section>

  <h3 class="investment-section-heading">Vocabulary Bank</h3>
  <section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
    <ul>
      <li><strong>raid</strong>: attacking visit into opponent half</li>
      <li><strong>raider</strong>: attacking player</li>
      <li><strong>tackle</strong>: defensive stop</li>
      <li><strong>bonus point</strong>: extra point available under conditions</li>
      <li><strong>all-out</strong>: all players of one side eliminated</li>
      <li><strong>do-or-die raid</strong>: raid where failure has extra cost</li>

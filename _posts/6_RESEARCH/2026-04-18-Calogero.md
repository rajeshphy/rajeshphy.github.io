We study a two-degree-of-freedom model with a velocity-coupling term and an inverse-square interaction. The classical dynamics becomes transparent after a change of variables that separates a conserved ‚Äúdrift-like‚Äù quantity from an Ermakov‚ÄìPinney-type radial equation. Quantization in the same variables leads to a solvable singular-oscillator equation whose normalizability requires contour (Stokes-wedge) boundary conditions rather than the real axis.

## Classical Dynamics and Hamiltonian Structure

Start from the Lagrangian

$$
L=\dot{x}\dot{y}+\omega(x\dot{y}-y\dot{x})-\omega^2xy-\frac{\epsilon}{2}(x^2+y^2)-\frac{g}{2(x-y)^2}.
$$

The conjugate momenta are

$$
P_x=\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{x}}=\dot{y}-\omega y,\qquad
P_y=\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{y}}=\dot{x}+\omega x.
$$

Eliminating $\dot{x},\dot{y}$ gives the Hamiltonian

$$
H=P_xP_y+\omega(yP_y-xP_x)+(\omega^2-\epsilon)xy+\frac{g}{2(x-y)^2}+\frac{\epsilon}{2}(x^2+y^2).
$$

The Euler‚ÄìLagrange equations yield coupled second-order dynamics with a singular inverse-cube force:

$$
\ddot{x}+2\omega\dot{x}+\omega^2x+\epsilon y+\frac{g}{(x-y)^3}=0,
$$

$$
\ddot{y}-2\omega\dot{y}+\omega^2y+\epsilon x-\frac{g}{(x-y)^3}=0.
$$

Introduce symmetric/antisymmetric variables

$$
z_1=x+y,\qquad z_2=x-y.
$$

Then the equations become

$$
\ddot{z}_1+(\omega^2+\epsilon)z_1+2\omega\dot{z}_2=0,
$$

$$
\ddot{z}_2+(\omega^2-\epsilon)z_2+2\omega\dot{z}_1+\frac{2g}{z_2^3}=0.
$$

A key gain is conceptual: $z_2$ carries the singular interaction, while $z_1$ couples only through first derivatives, so conserved combinations can emerge under special parameter choices.

## Rational Calogero Reduction and Integrability

For the rational Calogero case,

$$
\epsilon=-\omega^2,
$$

the system simplifies to

$$
\ddot{z}_1+2\omega\dot{z}_2=0,
$$

$$
\ddot{z}_2+2\omega^2 z_2+2\omega\dot{z}_1+\frac{2g}{z_2^3}=0.
$$

The first equation integrates once to a conserved quantity

$$
\Pi=\dot{z}_1+2\omega z_2=\text{constant}.
$$

Using $\dot{z}_1=\Pi-2\omega z_2$ in the second equation gives a single forced Ermakov‚ÄìPinney-type equation for $z_2$:

$$
\ddot{z}_2+\Omega^2 z_2+\frac{2g}{z_2^3}=-2\omega\Pi,
$$

with $\Omega$ an effective frequency determined by the reduced coefficients in the $z_2$ equation. In particular, when

$$
\Pi=0,
$$

one obtains the standard Ermakov‚ÄìPinney form

$$
\ddot{z}_2+\Omega^2 z_2+\frac{2g}{z_2^3}=0.
$$

A convenient explicit solution can be written in the amplitude-squared form

$$
z_2(t)=\left[\frac{1}{b^2\Omega^2}\left((a^2b^2-2g)\sin^2(\Omega t)+\frac{2ab}{\Omega}\sin(\Omega t)\cos(\Omega t)+b^2\cos^2(\Omega t)\right)\right]^{\frac{1}{2}}.
$$

Once $z_2(t)$ is known, $z_1(t)$ follows from the conserved $\Pi$:

$$
z_1(t)=\int\left(\Pi-2\omega z_2(t)\right)\,dt+I.
$$

For $\Pi=0$, the integral typically reduces to an elliptic integral of the second kind, so $z_1(t)$ is naturally expressed through an elliptic function $E(\phi,k^2)$. The classical system is therefore integrable in the Liouville sense with conserved quantities $H$ and $\Pi$, and the reduction shows explicitly how the singular interaction enters only through the $z_2$ sector.

## Quantum Reduction, Spectrum, and Stokes-Wedge Normalization

Quantization in the $(z_1,z_2)$ variables is imposed via the canonical commutators

$$
[z_1,P_{z_1}]=i,\qquad [z_2,P_{z_2}]=i,
$$

with the Schr√∂dinger representation

$$
P_{z_1}=-i\partial_{z_1},\qquad P_{z_2}=-i\partial_{z_2}.
$$

The Hamiltonian operator takes the form

$$
\hat{H}=-(\partial_{z_1}^2-\partial_{z_2}^2)+i\omega(z_1\partial_{z_2}+z_2\partial_{z_1})-\frac{\omega^2}{2}z_2^2+\frac{g}{2z_2^2}-\frac{\omega^2}{4}(z_1^2-z_2^2).
$$

A conserved operator corresponding to the classical constant $\Pi$ is

$$
\hat{\Pi}=-2i\frac{\partial}{\partial z_1}+\omega z_2.
$$

To separate variables, take $\psi$ to be an eigenfunction of $\hat{\Pi}$ with eigenvalue $k$:

$$
\psi=\exp\left[\frac{i}{2}z_1(k-\omega z_2)\right]\tilde{\phi}(z_2).
$$

Substitution into the Schr√∂dinger equation $\hat{H}\psi=E\psi$ reduces the problem to a single ordinary differential equation for $\tilde{\phi}(z_2)$:

$$
\tilde{\phi}''-\frac{1}{4}\Omega^2 z_2^2\tilde{\phi}+\frac{g}{2z_2^2}\tilde{\phi}+\frac{k}{4}(k-2\omega z_2)\tilde{\phi}=E\tilde{\phi}.
$$

For the simplest sector $k=0$, this becomes a singular-oscillator equation

$$
\tilde{\phi}''-\frac{1}{4}\Omega^2 z_2^2\tilde{\phi}+\frac{g}{2z_2^2}\tilde{\phi}=E\tilde{\phi}.
$$

A ground-state ansatz consistent with the inverse-square singularity and Gaussian asymptotics is

$$
\tilde{\phi}_0=z_2^\lambda e^{-C z_2^2},
$$

leading to

$$
E_0=-(2+4\lambda)C,
$$

with parameter constraints

$$
\lambda(\lambda-1)=-\frac{g}{2},\qquad C=\pm\frac{\Omega}{4}.
$$

A systematic series solution is obtained by

$$
\tilde{\phi}(z_2)=z_2^\lambda e^{-\frac{1}{4}\Omega z_2^2}\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}a_n z_2^n,
$$

with recursion relation

$$
a_{n+2}=\frac{(E+2C+4\lambda C)+4Cn}{(n+2)(n+1+2\lambda)}a_n.
$$

Polynomial termination of the series yields a discrete spectrum

$$
E=-2C(2n+1+2\lambda).
$$

A crucial physical point is that the resulting wavefunctions are generally not normalizable on the real axis. Instead, normalization is defined along complex contours (Stokes wedges) chosen so that:
- the exponential factor decays at infinity,
- the physical states remain bounded,
- the inner product becomes well-defined in the selected complex domain.

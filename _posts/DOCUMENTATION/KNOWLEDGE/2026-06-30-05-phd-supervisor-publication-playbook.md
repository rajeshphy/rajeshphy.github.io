A PhD supervisor is not a paper-production manager. The supervisor trains a researcher to ask defensible questions, keep a trustworthy record, make independent judgments, survive criticism, correct mistakes and communicate results to the right community. Publications are evidence of this development, not its sole purpose.

<div class="note-box" markdown="1">
**Supervision principle:** Never ask first, “Which Q1 journal can take this?” Ask, “What exactly have we established, how do we know, what would falsify it, who needs the result, and what is the most honest complete paper we can write?”
</div>

## 1. What a publication-ready PhD scholar should eventually do independently

By the later part of the PhD, the scholar should be able to:

- define a research question narrow enough to answer and important enough to matter;
- reconstruct the relevant literature rather than merely collect citations;
- distinguish an assumption, definition, derivation, numerical observation and physical conclusion;
- test limiting cases, dimensions, symmetries, sign conventions and known results;
- preserve enough code and reasoning for another researcher to audit the work;
- state novelty in one precise paragraph without exaggerated words;
- identify the paper's natural readers and prepare a reasoned journal shortlist;
- explain the difference between scope, selectivity, indexing and metrics;
- draft a cover letter and respond to referees professionally;
- identify a conflict of interest or unethical request;
- accept correction without treating it as humiliation; and
- maintain accurate ORCID, preprint and publication records.

The early PhD is supervised execution. The mature PhD is supervised judgment.

## 2. Begin with a written group publication charter

Discuss this charter in the first month and revisit it annually. It should be short enough that everyone reads it.

### Suggested charter

**Research record**

- Every result must have a dated derivation, notebook, source file or code commit.
- Raw calculations are preserved; presentation-ready plots do not replace source data.
- Numerical and symbolic software versions, parameters and assumptions are recorded.

**Authorship**

- Authorship follows qualifying intellectual contribution and accountability, not rank.
- Author order and [CRediT roles](https://credit.niso.org/) are discussed early and reviewed when contributions change.
- No manuscript or preprint is submitted until every author approves the exact files.

**Journal selection**

- Scope and audience are evaluated before metrics.
- Indexing, quartile, year, category, access model and charges are independently verified.
- The group submits to only one journal at a time.

**Writing and AI**

- Every author must be able to defend every claim and citation bearing their name.
- Substantive AI use is recorded and disclosed under the current journal policy.
- Confidential manuscripts and referee reports are not placed in unauthorized external tools.

**Peer review**

- Referee criticism is answered point by point with evidence.
- Reviewer requests are not obeyed when they damage correctness, relevance or ethics; disagreements are explained to the editor.

**Errors**

- Suspected errors are reported internally immediately without retaliation.
- The group corrects the public record promptly when an error affects a published claim.

**Assessment**

- No scholar is judged by one rejection, one journal name, a raw paper count or h-index alone.
- Originality, rigour, openness, contribution and intellectual growth are documented directly.

This charter gives a scholar permission to raise problems before they become misconduct.

## 3. Build a research programme, not isolated papers

A coherent PhD has a central question and a sequence of contributions. Map the thesis before chasing journals.

### The programme map

| Element | Supervisor question | Scholar's evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Domain | What physical system or formal problem is the thesis about? | One-paragraph domain statement |
| Gap | What is unknown, inconsistent or computationally inaccessible? | Annotated literature map |
| Core question | What would a successful thesis answer? | One testable central question |
| Work packages | Which analytical, numerical or conceptual steps answer it? | Dependency diagram and milestones |
| Validation | What known limits, alternative methods or independent checks apply? | Validation plan before results |
| Outputs | Which distinct scientific claims may deserve papers? | Provisional paper map, not a quota |
| Synthesis | What new understanding emerges across the outputs? | Thesis narrative updated yearly |

One work package may yield no paper but prevent an error; another may become two genuinely distinct papers. Do not promise an exact publication count before knowing the science.

## 4. Use six readiness gates before writing “the paper”

### Gate 1: question

The scholar can state:

- the physical question;
- why it is unresolved;
- the observable, theorem or inference that would answer it; and
- what lies outside the claim.

If the answer is merely “apply method X to potential Y,” ask what physical insight is gained and why the case matters.

### Gate 2: novelty

Require a novelty comparison:

For each closely related paper or preprint, record:

- its full citation;
- its assumptions, method and principal result;
- the assumption, method or question changed in the new work;
- the genuinely new result that follows; and
- why that difference matters physically or mathematically.

Novel algebra is not automatically new physics. A parameter substitution, routine potential, unmotivated fractional operator or new exact solution needs a physical or mathematical consequence beyond formal novelty.

### Gate 3: correctness

For theoretical quantum work, use an explicit audit:

- dimensions and units;
- Hermiticity, domains and boundary conditions;
- normalization and probability conservation;
- symmetry and conservation laws;
- signs, factors of \(2\), \(\pi\), \(\hbar\) and \(c\);
- limiting cases and known special solutions;
- perturbative order and approximation regime;
- regularization and renormalization conventions where relevant;
- numerical convergence and stability;
- coordinate, gauge or basis dependence; and
- consistency between abstract, equations, plots and conclusion.

Assign one coauthor to perform an **adversarial derivation audit** without using the main author's intermediate steps.

### Gate 4: reproducibility

Another group member should reproduce one central figure, table or calculation from the stored instructions. If this cannot be done internally, the paper is not ready for an external reader.

### Gate 5: interpretation

The scholar must distinguish:

- what the equations prove;
- what numerical evidence supports;
- what is physically plausible but untested;
- what is compared with prior work; and
- what remains speculative.

Ask the scholar to write the strongest valid conclusion and then one sentence describing its sharpest limitation.

### Gate 6: communication

Before journal formatting, prepare:

1. one-sentence result;
2. 150-word technical abstract;
3. one figure or equation carrying the central message;
4. three closest prior papers and the exact difference; and
5. a five-minute blackboard explanation for a neighbouring theorist.

If these disagree, the manuscript does not yet have a stable message.

## 5. The manuscript development cycle

Do not ask a scholar to “write everything and send it to me.” Use controlled passes.

### Pass 0: argument skeleton

Create only headings and one sentence per paragraph:

- problem and stakes;
- precise gap;
- approach and assumptions;
- central result;
- validation;
- implications and limitations.

### Pass 1: equations and evidence

Insert the derivation, theorem, computation, figures and checks. Every displayed equation should have a purpose in the argument. Move routine algebra to an appendix or reproducible supplement when it obstructs the logic.

### Pass 2: literature position

For every major claim, decide whether it is:

- common knowledge requiring a canonical source;
- a statement derived in the paper;
- a result taken from a specific source; or
- an interpretation that must be labelled as such.

### Pass 3: reader design

Rewrite the title, abstract, introduction and conclusion for the intended journal's audience. A broad journal needs cross-field significance; a mathematical-physics journal needs exact hypotheses and advance; a HEP journal needs the field-theoretic or phenomenological consequence.

### Pass 4: internal referee

Give the complete paper to someone who did not perform the main calculation. Ask for a report under the target journal's criteria, not line editing.

### Pass 5: submission package

Freeze a tagged source version containing manuscript, figures, bibliography, code/data release, cover letter, declarations, author approval and journal due-diligence sheet.

## 6. Teach journal choice as a scored argument

Use the detailed [journal landscape module]({{ '/quantum-physics-journal-landscape/' | relative_url }}) to create 3--6 candidates, then score them. A score starts discussion; it does not make the decision automatically.

| Criterion | Weight | Evidence required |
|---|---:|---|
| Exact scope and article-type fit | 25 | Official scope plus five recent comparable papers |
| Natural audience | 20 | Where the 20 closest papers and likely readers are located |
| Match to editorial significance threshold | 15 | Honest classification: landmark, major subfield, substantial specialist or incremental |
| Trust, active indexing and preservation | 15 | Official database records, ISSN and journal policies |
| Format and presentation fit | 8 | Length, letter/article, mathematical detail, supplements |
| Access, licence and affordability | 7 | No-fee route, APC, waiver, agreement, repository rights |
| Review quality and practical timing | 5 | Transparent process and credible dated statistics, if available |
| Contextual metrics | 5 | Database, metric, data year, category, rank and quartile |
| **Total** | **100** | Written decision |

Score each criterion from 0 to 5 and calculate:

\[
\text{weighted score}=\sum_i w_i\frac{s_i}{5}.
\]

Any journal failing trust or scope should be removed regardless of total.

### Reach, target and fallback

- **Reach:** plausible only if the broad significance claim survives hostile reading.
- **Target:** strongest fit for the paper's actual contribution and natural audience.
- **Fallback:** credible venue requiring little change in scientific framing, not merely the journal with the lowest metric.

Prepare all three before initial submission. Do not format three versions; know the routes and why they differ.

## 7. Run publication meetings with evidence

### Weekly research meeting

The scholar submits one page before the meeting:

```text
Question this week:
Result or obstruction:
Evidence produced:
Check that failed:
Decision required from supervisor:
Next falsifiable task:
Files/commit where the work is recorded:
```

Spend the meeting on reasoning and decisions, not reconstructing undocumented activity.

### Monthly literature meeting

The scholar presents one recent paper and one older foundational paper:

- central claim;
- assumptions;
- method;
- strongest evidence;
- limitation;
- relation to the thesis; and
- journal/audience fit.

This teaches both physics and journal culture.

### Quarterly publication review

Review the programme, not just deadlines:

- Which claims became stronger or weaker?
- Which proposed paper should be merged, delayed or abandoned?
- Are authorship roles still accurate?
- Has the target community shifted?
- Are records reproducible?
- Is any policy or university requirement approaching?

## 8. Make authorship discussion normal

At project start, fill this record:

```text
Project:
Provisional contributors:
Expected CRediT roles for each person:
Provisional author order and rationale:
Corresponding-author responsibilities:
What contribution is still required for authorship:
Date of next review:
Disagreement-resolution route:
```

The record is provisional. If someone stops contributing, discuss the change early. If a new collaborator performs decisive formal analysis or validation, update the record. Never use authorship as a surprise reward or punishment.

### A useful author approval ritual

Before preprint and journal submission, every author replies to the same message confirming:

1. they read the complete version;
2. their name, affiliation and ORCID are correct;
3. their contribution statement is accurate;
4. conflicts and funding are declared;
5. they approve the target journal and access route; and
6. they accept accountability for their contribution and will help resolve integrity questions.

Silence is not approval.

## 9. Train peer-review skill before the first submission

A scholar who can review a paper can revise one intelligently.

### Internal review exercise

Choose a published paper close to the project. Hide its journal and outcome initially. Ask the scholar to write:

- a neutral two-sentence summary;
- the main contribution;
- two major strengths;
- major issues affecting validity or significance;
- minor issues affecting clarity;
- missing literature or tests;
- a recommendation under a stated journal's criteria; and
- what evidence would change the recommendation.

Then compare with the published version, corrections and, where public, referee reports. The goal is not to guess accept/reject; it is to separate correctness, novelty, importance and presentation.

### Review ethics

Teach the scholar to decline when expertise or time is inadequate, disclose conflicts, protect confidentiality, avoid using unpublished ideas, write constructively, and never demand irrelevant self-citations. See the [COPE Core Practices](https://publicationethics.org/core-practices) and the target publisher's current reviewer policy.

## 10. The referee-response protocol

When reports arrive, do not reply immediately.

### Day 1: classify

Mark every point as:

- validity/correctness;
- missing evidence or comparison;
- scope/significance;
- clarity/presentation;
- policy/format;
- optional suggestion; or
- request requiring editorial guidance.

### Day 2--7: reproduce before rebutting

For technical criticism, rerun the derivation or computation. A confident memory is not evidence. Record whether the referee found a real error, a missing explanation or a misunderstanding created by the manuscript.

### Response structure

For each numbered comment:

1. quote or accurately summarize the concern;
2. state whether you agree, partly agree or disagree;
3. describe the action or evidence;
4. give the exact manuscript location; and
5. explain why the revision resolves the concern.

Example:

> **Comment 2:** The approximation near Eq. (18) appears invalid when \(g/\omega\sim1\).
>
> **Response:** We agree that the original text did not state the regime clearly. We re-derived the expansion and now require \(g/\omega\ll1\), added the neglected next-order term, and compared it numerically in new Fig. 3. The conclusion is unchanged within the stated regime but is no longer claimed at ultrastrong coupling. These changes appear in Sec. III, paragraphs 2--3, and Appendix B.

This is stronger than “corrected as suggested.”

### When to disagree

Disagree when a request would introduce an error, misrepresent the scope, require irrelevant work or violate ethics. Use calculations and sources, not status. If the issue is unresolved between reports, ask the editor a narrow question.

### Appeals

Appeal only for a material procedural error, factual misunderstanding demonstrable from the submitted record, unmanaged conflict, or decision inconsistent with stated policy. An appeal is not a second cover letter saying the work is important.

## 11. Use rejection as diagnosis

### Desk rejection

Possible diagnoses:

- outside scope;
- correct topic but wrong audience;
- significance below the journal's threshold;
- novelty not visible in title/abstract/cover letter;
- unsuitable length or article type; or
- fatal presentation/trust issue.

Read the editor's language precisely. A broad-selective rejection may require a different journal and framing, not more algebra.

### Rejection after review

Create a decision memo:

```text
Decision and date:
Editor’s main reason:
Valid technical criticisms:
Criticisms we dispute and evidence:
New work required:
Claims that must be narrowed:
Manuscript changes before any resubmission:
Revised journal shortlist:
Scholar’s lessons for the next project:
```

Do not send an unchanged paper to the fallback within hours. Fix what is valid first, even if a new journal will not see the old report.

### Emotional supervision

Separate three sentences:

- “This journal declined this version.”
- “This report found a weakness in this argument.”
- “You are becoming a poor researcher.”

Only the first two may be supported by the decision. The third is harmful and normally false.

## 12. Evaluate the scholar responsibly

[DORA](https://sfdora.org/read/) advises against using journal metrics as surrogates for an individual paper or researcher. Apply that principle in annual reviews.

### Evidence portfolio

Assess:

- quality and originality of questions;
- command of literature;
- correctness and transparency of reasoning;
- ability to reproduce and validate;
- intellectual contribution using CRediT roles;
- manuscripts, preprints, code, data and negative results;
- quality of seminar and written explanation;
- response to criticism and correction;
- collaboration and ethical conduct;
- growing independence; and
- actual reception of outputs, interpreted in field and time context.

Journal name, citations and h-index may provide context, but they do not replace this evidence.

### Bad targets

Avoid group quotas such as:

- “two Q1 papers per year”;
- “impact factor total of 10”;
- “h-index before thesis submission”; or
- “paper must be accepted in three months.”

These targets invite premature submission, fragmentation, unsafe journals, coercive authorship and topic selection for citation potential rather than scientific value.

### Better process indicators

- percentage of central calculations independently checked;
- completeness of research records;
- time from finding an error to documenting it;
- proportion of references actually verified;
- internal review completed before submission;
- transparent authorship records;
- code/data release readiness; and
- increasing number of decisions the scholar can justify independently.

## 13. Maintain a small publication dashboard

Use a spreadsheet or versioned YAML/CSV file with one row per manuscript:

| Field | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Project and claim | Prevent title-driven tracking from hiding the science |
| Scholar and CRediT roles | Make contribution visible |
| Readiness gate | Show the current scientific bottleneck |
| Preprint/version | Preserve public and internal version history |
| Reach/target/fallback | Record strategy before emotion enters |
| Journal evidence date | Prevent stale metric/index claims |
| Submission and decision dates | Measure process without inventing promises |
| Decision diagnosis | Turn rejection into group knowledge |
| Data/code archive | Support reproducibility |
| DOI/correction status | Maintain the record after publication |

Never rank scholars publicly by papers or citations.

## 14. A 90-day implementation plan

### Days 1--15: establish the foundation

- Discuss and sign the publication charter.
- Ensure every member has one authenticated [ORCID iD](https://support.orcid.org/hc/en-us/articles/360006897334-What-is-an-ORCID-iD-and-how-do-I-use-it).
- Create standard project, authorship, journal-audit and referee-response templates.
- Read the current university PhD publication requirements and archive them with the access date.

### Days 16--30: map the science

- Build the thesis programme map and annotated literature map.
- Identify the nearest 20 papers for each active claim.
- Classify outputs as thesis chapter, possible paper, technical note, negative result or work still too preliminary.

### Days 31--45: audit reproducibility

- Reproduce one central result without the original author's hidden steps.
- Tag software versions and establish a release folder.
- Write honest data/code availability statements.

### Days 46--60: learn journal judgment

- Complete the metrics and journal-landscape modules together.
- Score candidate journals for one real manuscript.
- Verify official indexing, scope, fees, preprint and AI policies.

### Days 61--75: practise peer review

- Run the published-paper internal review exercise.
- Exchange mock reports within the group.
- Revise the manuscript from evidence, not deference.

### Days 76--90: conduct a submission-readiness review

- Pass all six gates.
- Freeze the author-approved preprint/submission package.
- Complete due diligence and choose the target in writing.
- Set a plan for arXiv, submission, response ownership and post-publication updates.

## 15. Questions for every supervision milestone

### At project start

- What observation or theoretical tension motivates this?
- What result would make us abandon the approach?
- Who owns which task and record?
- What authorship contribution is expected?

### At first result

- Can it be reproduced from stored files?
- Which known limit is recovered?
- Is the result new, or only new to us?
- What physical inference is justified?

### At manuscript start

- What is the single claim?
- Which three papers are closest?
- What evidence is still missing?
- Who is the exact reader?

### Before submission

- Have all authors read and approved this version?
- What would cause a desk rejection?
- What is the dated proof of scope, indexing, quartile and fees?
- Can we defend every reference, equation and figure?
- What is the fallback and what would we revise first?

### After publication

- Is the DOI connected to ORCID and the preprint?
- Are code and data links permanent and correct?
- Did the published proof introduce an error?
- What did review teach us about the next project?
- Has any new evidence weakened the result?

## Final perspective

The scholar should not leave the PhD knowing only how to place a paper. They should know how to decide whether a result deserves a paper, how to expose it to the right criticism, how to preserve evidence, how to share credit fairly, and how to protect the literature when something goes wrong.

That is journal intelligence: not memorising Q1 lists, but understanding the institutions, incentives and responsibilities through which physics becomes part of the scientific record.

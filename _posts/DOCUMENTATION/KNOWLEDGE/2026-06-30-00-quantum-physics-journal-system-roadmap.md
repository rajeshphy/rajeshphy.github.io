Scientific journals form the principal certification and archival system for research literature. In quantum and theoretical physics, that system includes specialist and broad journals, editorial offices, peer reviewers, bibliographic databases, citation indicators, repositories such as arXiv, research identifiers, open-access arrangements, and institutional assessment rules.

This series is an **awareness-oriented reference** for research groups. It describes the vocabulary, institutions and processes of journal publishing without reducing publication to a collection of targets or scores. The later modules place publishing inside the wider research system: active institutions, international mobility, scientific meetings, collaborations, durable professional networks, the funding structures that support research in Indian colleges, and the institutional organization of seminars, workshops, schools and conferences.

**Q1, Q2, Q3 and Q4 are quartiles, not calendar quarters.** A quartile is a journal's relative position within a specified subject category, database, metric and data year. A statement such as “the journal is Q1” is therefore incomplete without that context.
</div>

## The main layers of the journal system

Several related systems are often mistaken for one another.

| Layer | Examples | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Journal | Physical Review A, Annals of Physics, JHEP | Selects, reviews, edits, publishes and preserves articles for a defined readership |
| Publisher or learned society | APS, IOP Publishing, Elsevier, Springer Nature | Operates journals, platforms, policies and production services |
| Bibliographic database | Web of Science Core Collection, Scopus, INSPIRE-HEP, NASA ADS | Indexes selected records and supports discovery and citation analysis |
| Analytics product | Journal Citation Reports, Scopus Sources, SCImago | Organizes journal-level citation information |
| Metric | JIF, JCI, CiteScore, SJR, SNIP, h-index | Summarizes one aspect of citation activity under a defined method |
| Repository | arXiv, institutional repositories | Disseminates author manuscripts and preserves versions outside the journal platform |
| Persistent identifier | DOI, ORCID, ISSN | Identifies articles, researchers and serial publications |
| Ethics or assessment framework | COPE, CRediT, DORA, Leiden Manifesto | Defines principles for integrity, contributorship and responsible evaluation |
| Institutional rule | University ordinances, funder mandates, national guidance | Establishes local administrative requirements |

Indexing does not mean that a database published or peer reviewed an article. A metric does not certify the correctness of a paper. Open access describes availability and licensing rather than scientific quality. These distinctions explain many apparent contradictions in journal discussions.

## Journal identity and scope

A journal is identified by its title and ISSN, together with its publisher and publication history. Its stated scope defines the communities and article types it is intended to serve. Scope can be broad, as in a general physics journal, or narrow, as in journals devoted to quantum information, mathematical physics, high-energy phenomenology or gravitation.

Three well-known titles illustrate different editorial homes:

- **Annals of Physics** is associated with substantial and fully developed work in basic theoretical physics, often allowing extended derivations and first-principles treatments.
- **International Journal of Modern Physics A** mainly serves particles and fields, with related work in gravitation and cosmology.
- **The European Physical Journal Plus** has a broad remit across fundamental, applied and interdisciplinary physics.

These descriptions are differences of scope and readership, not positions on a single universal ladder.

## Quartiles and rankings

Quartiles divide an ordered subject-category list into four broad regions:

- **Q1:** upper quarter;
- **Q2:** second quarter;
- **Q3:** third quarter;
- **Q4:** fourth quarter.

The underlying ordering may be based on Journal Impact Factor in Journal Citation Reports, CiteScore percentile in Scopus, or SJR in SCImago. These systems use different source collections, subject categories, time windows and calculations. A journal may consequently be Q1 in one database, Q2 in another, or have different quartiles in two categories during the same year. Tied metric values can also make the four groups unequal in size.

Quartiles describe relative journal-level citation position. They do not express the truth of an article, the difficulty of its calculation, the quality of its peer review, or the importance of an individual researcher's contribution.

## The major citation indicators

The **Journal Impact Factor (JIF)** measures citations in a given year to selected items published during the preceding two years, divided by the number of citable items in that two-year window. It is reported in Journal Citation Reports and is sensitive to field, article type and citation time scale.

The **Journal Citation Indicator (JCI)** is a field-normalized journal measure. A value of 1 represents the world average for comparable publications under its normalization model.

**CiteScore** uses Scopus data and a four-year publication and citation window with a symmetrical set of document types. **SJR** gives greater weight to citations from influential sources. **SNIP** normalizes citation impact using the citation characteristics of the source's subject field.

An **h-index** of \(h\) means that \(h\) items have each received at least \(h\) citations. The index can be calculated for a researcher or a journal. Its value depends on database coverage, field, career length and publication history, so values from Scopus, Web of Science and Google Scholar are not directly interchangeable.

All of these indicators compress complex citation distributions into single numbers. Review articles, large fields, older journals and rapidly publishing communities generally accumulate citations differently from narrow, young or slow-moving fields.

## What “top journal” can mean

The expression **top journal** has no single technical definition. It may refer to:

- broad visibility across physics;
- high editorial selectivity;
- strong influence inside a specialty;
- a high current citation indicator;
- historical reputation;
- exceptional mathematical rigor; or
- a journal read routinely by the community relevant to a paper.

Nature Physics, Physical Review X and Physical Review Letters are prominent across broad areas of physics. PRX Quantum, npj Quantum Information and Quantum Science and Technology are major names in quantum information science. Physical Review A, Quantum and New Journal of Physics serve important parts of quantum physics. Communications in Mathematical Physics, Annales Henri Poincaré, Journal of Mathematical Physics and Journal of Physics A occupy different parts of mathematical and theoretical physics. JHEP, Physical Review D, European Physical Journal C, Nuclear Physics B and JCAP serve distinct high-energy, field-theory and cosmology communities.

Review journals such as Reviews of Modern Physics and Reports on Progress in Physics have a different function from ordinary original-research journals and often display unusually high citation averages.

## The publication lifecycle

A journal article normally passes through several institutional states:

1. **Author manuscript:** the research group prepares the argument, equations, figures, references and declarations.
2. **Submission:** files and metadata enter a journal's editorial system.
3. **Administrative checks:** staff examine completeness, formatting and policy compliance.
4. **Editorial assessment:** an editor considers scope, originality, significance and suitability for external review.
5. **Peer review:** external specialists evaluate the claims and evidence.
6. **Decision:** the journal may reject, request minor or major revision, invite resubmission, or accept.
7. **Production:** accepted text is copyedited, typeset, proofed and assigned publication metadata.
8. **Version of record:** the publisher releases the formal citable article.
9. **Post-publication record:** corrections, expressions of concern or retractions may modify the record when necessary.

Peer review is an expert evaluation process, not a proof that every statement is permanently correct. Editors make publication decisions, while reviewers usually provide recommendations rather than final decisions.

## Access and manuscript versions

Subscription journals place the version of record behind institutional or personal access, although they may permit repository versions. **Gold open access** makes the version of record openly available, often under a Creative Commons licence and sometimes with an article processing charge. **Hybrid journals** combine subscription publication with an optional open-access route. **Diamond open access** charges neither readers nor authors. **Green open access** refers to repository access to a permitted manuscript version.

In high-energy physics, **SCOAP³** centrally supports open access for participating journals and eligible articles. The arrangement illustrates how costs can be coordinated through institutions rather than charged directly to individual authors.

The three important manuscript states are the **preprint**, the **author accepted manuscript**, and the publisher's **version of record**. Their text may be similar, but their legal status, formatting, peer-review status and permitted distribution can differ.

## Integrity and responsibility

Publication ethics covers authorship, contributorship, originality, citation, conflicts of interest, confidentiality, data and code, image integrity, corrections and the conduct of peer review. CRediT supplies a vocabulary for contributor roles, while COPE describes core practices for journals and publishers.

Fabrication, falsification and plagiarism are the most familiar forms of research misconduct. Duplicate submission, undisclosed text recycling, manipulated citations, paper fragmentation, guest authorship and hidden conflicts can also distort the scientific record.

Generative-AI policies are publisher-specific and continue to change. Common principles include the exclusion of AI systems from authorship, continuing human accountability, disclosure of substantive uses, verification of generated material and protection of confidential manuscripts and referee reports.

## PhD supervision within this system

For a PhD scholar, publication is both a research outcome and a training environment. It exposes the scholar to literature evaluation, authorship responsibility, editorial judgment, criticism, revision and record keeping. The supervisor has greater authority and experience, which makes transparency about credit, assessment and journal choice important to the integrity of the group.

Responsible research assessment frameworks such as DORA and the Leiden Manifesto distinguish the quality of a research contribution from the citation reputation of the journal that contains it. This distinction is especially important in doctoral training, where independence, correctness, methodological understanding and response to error are educational outcomes that cannot be reduced to publication counts or quartiles.

## Indian regulatory context

The University Grants Commission discontinued the UGC-CARE journal listing and issued a public notice in February 2025 describing suggestive parameters for evaluating peer-reviewed journals. These parameters concern journal identity, editorial transparency, peer-review information, fees, ethics, access, archiving and related features. The former list is therefore not a permanently updated whitelist.

University PhD regulations, employment rules and funding conditions remain separate administrative layers. A journal may be scientifically established while a particular institution imposes additional documentary requirements.

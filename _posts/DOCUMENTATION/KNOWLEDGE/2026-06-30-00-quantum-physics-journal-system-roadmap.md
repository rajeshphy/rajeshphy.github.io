This is a working course in **journal literacy** for theoretical-physics researchers, research groups and supervisors of PhD scholars. It is not a list of fashionable journal names. Its purpose is to make the whole publication system intelligible: who indexes journals, who calculates metrics, what Q1--Q4 actually mean, how editors decide, how to choose a journal, how to supervise authorship and peer review, and how to recognize bad incentives.

<div class="note-box" markdown="1">
**First correction:** Q1, Q2, Q3 and Q4 mean **quartiles**, not calendar quarters. A quartile is a journal's relative position inside a stated subject category, database, metric system and data year. Therefore, “this is a Q1 journal” is incomplete unless those four pieces of context are supplied.
</div>

The external facts and links in this series were checked on **30 June 2026**. Journal scopes, fees, editorial policies, indexing and metrics can change; the verification method taught here is more important than any frozen number.

## Three useful reference points

Three representative journals illustrate why publication venues should not be placed on one simple prestige ladder:

- **Annals of Physics**, a natural home for substantial and fully developed work across basic theoretical physics, often with room for long derivations and first-principles treatment;
- **International Journal of Modern Physics A**, whose core community is particles and fields, with selected work in gravitation and cosmology; and
- **The European Physical Journal Plus**, a broad physics journal spanning fundamental, applied and interdisciplinary work.

These journals represent different editorial homes rather than three rungs on one ladder. A research group should therefore build a **topic-dependent journal portfolio**, not simply try to “move to a higher impact factor.” A paper on quantum information, a rigorous spectral theorem, a short field-theory breakthrough, and a long modified-gravity calculation have different natural audiences and different credible target journals.

## What this course should enable you to do

After reading the full series, you should be able to:

1. state a journal quartile correctly, including its source, metric, year and category;
2. distinguish Journal Impact Factor, CiteScore, SJR, SNIP, JCI and h-index without mixing journal-, article- and author-level claims;
3. explain why the same journal can be Q1 in one category and Q2 in another;
4. construct a defensible three-journal shortlist for any theoretical-physics manuscript;
5. distinguish a highly selective journal from a reliable specialist journal;
6. recognize legitimate open access, hybrid publishing, green self-archiving and predatory imitation;
7. guide a scholar from research question through manuscript, preprint, submission, revision and post-publication correction;
8. agree authorship and contributions before conflict develops;
9. assess a scholar's work directly rather than using the journal metric as a substitute; and
10. maintain an annual, dated journal-intelligence record for your research group.

## The publication system in one map

The system has several layers. Confusion arises when one layer is mistaken for another.

| Layer | Examples | What it actually tells you | What it does **not** tell you |
|---|---|---|---|
| Journal and publisher | Physical Review A, Annals of Physics, JHEP; APS, Elsevier, Springer Nature | Scope, editorial criteria, peer-review process, article types, access model and author policy | That every published paper is equally important |
| Bibliographic index | Web of Science Core Collection, Scopus, INSPIRE-HEP, ADS | Whether covered records and citations are searchable in that database | A universal guarantee of quality for all time |
| Analytics product | Journal Citation Reports, Scopus Sources, SCImago | A platform that reports defined journal and citation data | That differently named products or datasets are interchangeable |
| Journal metric | JIF, CiteScore, SJR, SNIP, JCI | One defined quantitative view of journal citation behaviour | The merit or correctness of an individual paper |
| Repository and identifier | arXiv, institutional repository, DOI, ORCID | Dissemination, version access and persistent identification | Peer-review certification by itself |
| Ethics and practice framework | COPE, DORA, CRediT, Think. Check. Submit. | Principles and tools for responsible publication and assessment | A substitute for reading the target journal's current policy |
| Institutional or national rule | University PhD ordinance, funder mandate, current UGC guidance | What is formally required in a particular administrative context | A timeless international ranking of journals |

The normal route is:

**research record → defensible result → manuscript → internal audit → journal shortlist → preprint if appropriate → one-journal submission → editorial screening → peer review → revision or rejection → acceptance → proof → version of record → indexing and discovery → correction if needed**.

Each arrow can fail. Good supervision makes the failures visible early, while they are still cheap to correct.

## Fifteen truths worth memorising

### 1. Q means quartile, and quartile is relative

Q1 is the upper group in a subject category; Q4 is the lower group. It is a position within a comparison set, not an absolute measurement of truth, rigour or suitability.

### 2. There is no context-free “Q1”

Web of Science/JCR and Scopus-based systems use different journal sets, category schemes and metrics. A title may occupy multiple categories. Always record **database + metric + data year + category**.

### 3. A journal metric is not an article metric

A high Journal Impact Factor says something about a journal-level citation average. It does not predict the citations, correctness or importance of one paper. Citation distributions inside journals are strongly uneven. [DORA explicitly advises against using journal metrics as a surrogate for an individual article or researcher](https://sfdora.org/read/).

### 4. Scope fit is the first gate

Editors ask whether the manuscript belongs to the journal before referees ask whether the mathematics is correct. A technically sound paper can be desk-rejected because its advance, audience, length or article type does not fit.

### 5. “Top” is field- and paper-dependent

Physical Review Letters, Physical Review X and Nature Physics seek broad, unusually consequential advances. Physical Review A, Physical Review D, JHEP, Journal of Physics A or Classical and Quantum Gravity may be more influential **for the exact community that needs the result**.

### 6. Selectivity and quality are different concepts

A journal can be rigorous, useful and respected without accepting only spectacular results. Conversely, a high metric cannot rescue poor scope fit or weak editorial practice.

### 7. Review journals are a different species

Reviews of Modern Physics, Physics Reports and similar venues primarily publish authoritative syntheses, often invited or proposal-led. They are not ordinary destinations for a standard original research paper.

### 8. Indexing is specific and can change

“Indexed” should mean indexed in a named database and index, verified at the database itself using the journal's ISSN. A journal website's logo is not proof. Coverage may begin, end or be re-evaluated.

### 9. Open access is an access model, not a quality grade

Excellent and poor journals exist under subscription, hybrid and open-access models. An article processing charge is a business mechanism; it should never purchase acceptance.

### 10. Free-to-publish and free-to-read are different

A subscription journal may charge no publication fee but restrict readers. A gold open-access journal may be free to read but charge authors. Diamond open access charges neither readers nor authors. Some HEP papers are made open through [SCOAP³](https://scoap3.org/what-is-scoap3/) without an author-facing APC.

### 11. A preprint is public scholarship, not journal certification

arXiv rapidly establishes a dated public version and invites community scrutiny. It normally has not received the target journal's peer review. The preprint, accepted manuscript and version of record must not be described as if they were identical.

### 12. Submission to two journals at once is normally unethical

Preprinting is usually compatible with physics journals, but simultaneous journal submission wastes editorial and reviewer labour and violates standard declarations.

### 13. Authorship is an accountability decision

Authorship is not a gift, favour or automatic consequence of seniority. Decide the author list and order through actual intellectual contributions, keep the decision revisable, and record roles using a framework such as [CRediT](https://credit.niso.org/).

### 14. Rejection is information, not a verdict on a career

Desk rejection often means audience or threshold mismatch. Referee rejection may reveal a fixable technical weakness or a disagreement. The correct response is to diagnose, revise and retarget—not to submit the unchanged paper repeatedly down a prestige ladder.

### 15. Correction is part of research integrity

An erratum or correction can be evidence that authors protect the record. Concealment is the problem. A group should have an explicit post-publication error protocol.

## The responsible way to describe a journal

Do not say:

> Journal X is Q1 and has impact factor 5, so it is a top journal.

Say something like:

> In the 2026 Journal Citation Reports, which reflect 2025 data, Journal X is in JIF quartile Q1 in category Y. Its scope and recent papers match topic Z. The research group separately checked its current Scopus coverage, access route, author charges and peer-review policy on 30 June 2026.

The second statement is dated, auditable and intellectually honest. It also leaves room for the decisive question: **will the right physicists read this paper there?**

## A journal decision should use five lenses

For every manuscript, examine these lenses in order:

1. **Scientific fit:** Does the journal publish this exact kind of question, method and level of advance?
2. **Audience fit:** Are the researchers who should use or challenge the result regular readers and authors there?
3. **Editorial threshold:** Is the claim incremental, substantial within a specialty, broadly important, or genuinely field-changing?
4. **Trust and logistics:** Is the journal verifiably indexed, ethically run, affordable, compatible with funder rules and realistically timed?
5. **Metrics in context:** What do the current category ranks and multiple indicators add after the first four questions are answered?

Metrics come fifth, not first.

## Your annual journal-intelligence routine

Run this audit each July after the major annual metrics updates, and again immediately before each submission.

### Annual audit

- Export or record the latest JCR and Scopus source information for the group's 15--25 plausible journals.
- Record the **data year**, release year, category, rank, percentile and quartile; never overwrite the previous year's record.
- Check whether any journal changed title, publisher, scope, indexing status, access model, APC, preprint policy or editorial leadership.
- Read the titles and abstracts of its latest 20 relevant papers.
- Mark review-only, letters-focused, broad-selective and specialist-workhorse journals separately.
- Review your own submissions: desk-rejection rate, review quality, time, cost and whether the intended audience actually engaged.
- Remove journals that no longer serve the group's science, even if their metric increased.

### Pre-submission audit

- Verify the title and ISSN in the [Web of Science Master Journal List](https://mjl.clarivate.com/home) and [Scopus Sources](https://www.scopus.com/sources), as relevant.
- Open the official journal site manually; do not submit through an email solicitation.
- Read the current aims, acceptance criteria, author guide, ethics policy, AI policy, preprint policy and charges.
- Find five papers from the previous two years that are genuinely comparable.
- Write one sentence explaining why the journal's readers need this result now.
- Keep a screenshot or PDF of crucial policies and metrics with the date checked.

## India-specific regulatory note

The UGC-CARE list is no longer a living approval list. The [official UGC-CARE site states that it has not been updated since October 2024 and will not be updated further](https://ugccare.unipune.ac.in/apps1/home/index), following the 11 February 2025 UGC notice. UGC moved toward suggestive parameters for selecting peer-reviewed journals, including transparent peer review, editorial-board credibility, fees, ethics, indexing and citation context.

For a PhD scholar, therefore, check three things separately:

1. the scholar's **current university PhD ordinance and thesis-submission rule**;
2. the journal's current, independently verified quality and indexing evidence; and
3. the scientific value and integrity of the paper itself.

An archived list cannot replace current due diligence, and an administrative minimum should never become the research group's quality ceiling.

## A short vocabulary before continuing

| Term | Working meaning |
|---|---|
| Journal | A continuing scholarly publication with an editorial and peer-review system |
| Publisher | The organisation providing publication infrastructure; it may be a society, university, nonprofit or company |
| Index | A database that selects and describes publications and often tracks their citations |
| Metric | A number calculated under a specified formula, dataset and time window |
| Rank | A journal's ordered position within a category under a named metric |
| Percentile | A standardized expression of relative position in that comparison set |
| Quartile | One of four rank bands, Q1 through Q4 |
| APC | Article processing charge, usually associated with an open-access route |
| DOI | Persistent identifier for a research output; not a certificate of quality |
| ORCID iD | Persistent person identifier that helps connect a researcher with outputs and affiliations |
| Preprint | Public manuscript shared before formal journal certification |
| Version of record | The final publisher-maintained journal version |
| Desk rejection | Editorial rejection before external peer review |
| Major revision | A decision requiring substantial changes and another evaluation; not an acceptance |

## The governing principle

The intelligent question is not:

> What is the highest-Q journal that may accept this paper?

It is:

> What claim have we established, which community needs it, what evidence would convince that community, and which trustworthy journal gives the work the most appropriate test and readership?

That question produces better papers, better journal decisions and better PhD supervision.

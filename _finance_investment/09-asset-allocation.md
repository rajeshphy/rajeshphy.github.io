 <h2 class="act-section-heading"><span>9</span>Asset Allocation</h2>
<p class="act-essence-box investment-intro">Asset allocation decides the growth-stability structure of the portfolio. The chapter begins with the equity-debt ratio, then compares age-based and goal-based allocation, and finally describes conservative, moderate, and aggressive portfolios.</p>
<figure class="investment-diagram-figure">
  <img src="{{ '/assets/finance/investment-09-asset-allocation.png' | relative_url }}" alt="Allocation controls portfolio behaviour">
  <figcaption>Allocation controls portfolio behaviour</figcaption>
</figure>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Equity-debt ratio</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> a 70:30 equity-debt portfolio behaves differently from a 30:70 portfolio during a crash. The ratio is the main control knob for volatility and long-term growth.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>The equity-debt ratio is the percentage split between growth assets and stabilizing assets.</p>
</section>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-theory">
  <h4>Dominant design variable</h4>
<p>Portfolio behavior is dominated more by allocation than by product selection. Indian equity supplies long-term growth; Indian debt supplies ballast.</p>
</section>
<p><strong>Choose ratio from goal and temperament.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Choose the ratio from goal horizon and risk tolerance.</li>
  <li>Increase debt as a fixed goal approaches.</li>
  <li>Rebalance when the ratio drifts materially.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Age-based allocation</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> a 28-year-old with stable income and no near goal may hold more equity than a 60-year-old beginning withdrawals. Age is a useful proxy because human capital and recovery time change.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Age-based allocation is a shortcut that reduces equity as age rises.</p>
</section>
<p>It is useful as a starting estimate because younger investors usually have more income years ahead. It is incomplete because income security, dependents, goals, and temperament also matter.</p>
<p><strong>Use age as input, not command.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Use age only as a starting estimate.</li>
  <li>Adjust for job stability and liabilities.</li>
  <li>Do not hold high equity merely because a formula says so.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Goal-based allocation</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> the same person can hold equity for retirement, debt for a three-year house goal, and cash for emergencies. Allocation should follow the date and importance of each goal.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Goal-based allocation assigns each goal its own risk profile and product set.</p>
</section>
<p>Retirement, house down payment, education fees, and emergency money should not share one blind allocation.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-law">
  <h4>Separate by goal</h4>
<ul>
  <li>Create separate buckets by goal and date.</li>
  <li>Match each bucket to its horizon.</li>
  <li>Review buckets independently.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Conservative portfolio</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> an investor supporting parents, paying EMI, and saving for a near goal may choose a debt-heavy portfolio. Lower return is acceptable if it prevents forced selling.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>A conservative portfolio prioritizes capital stability and lower drawdown.</p>
</section>
<p>It suits near goals, low risk tolerance, or uncertain income. It accepts lower expected return to reduce emotional and financial stress.</p>
<p><strong>Stability dominant.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Keep debt and cash dominant.</li>
  <li>Use limited equity only for inflation protection if horizon allows.</li>
  <li>Avoid products that can surprise on the downside.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Moderate portfolio</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> a middle-career salaried investor with emergency money and goals beyond five years may mix equity and debt. The portfolio seeks growth but keeps enough stability to stay invested.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>A moderate portfolio balances growth and stability.</p>
</section>
<p>It accepts meaningful volatility but avoids extreme dependence on equity. It suits investors with medium or long horizons and stable behavior.</p>
<p><strong>Balanced discipline.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Combine diversified equity with quality debt.</li>
  <li>Rebalance at defined intervals.</li>
  <li>Do not keep changing allocation with headlines.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Aggressive portfolio</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> an investor with high income stability, long horizon, and no near withdrawal need may accept high equity exposure. Aggression is justified only when drawdowns can be held without panic.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>An aggressive portfolio seeks higher long-term growth through high equity exposure.</p>
</section>
<p>It can fall deeply in bad markets. It works only if the investor has long horizon, stable income, and the ability to hold through fear.</p>
<p><strong>Aggression needs capacity.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Use aggressive allocation only for long-term goals.</li>
  <li>Prepare mentally for large drawdowns.</li>
  <li>Keep emergency and near-goal money outside it.</li>
</ul>

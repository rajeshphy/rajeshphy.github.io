 <h2 class="act-section-heading"><span>18</span>Investor Safety</h2>
<p class="act-essence-box investment-intro">Investor safety is operational discipline. This chapter covers KYC, demat accounts, brokers, nominees, password safety, fraud calls, fake tips, and SEBI investor protection.</p>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">KYC</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> mutual fund, broker, and demat access in India begins with PAN, Aadhaar-linked verification, bank details, and KYC records. Clean KYC prevents transaction failure when markets or personal needs demand action.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Know Your Customer is identity verification required for Indian financial transactions.</p>
</section>
<p>It protects the system from misuse and links investments to a verified person. Without KYC, investing operations may be blocked.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-law">
  <h4>Use official channels</h4>
<ul>
  <li>Complete KYC through official channels.</li>
  <li>Keep PAN, address, bank, and mobile details updated.</li>
  <li>Never share OTPs with anyone claiming to complete KYC.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Demat account</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> shares, ETFs, and many bonds sit in a demat account rather than paper certificates. The account is infrastructure; it should be with a reliable depository participant and secured carefully.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>A demat account holds Indian securities electronically through NSDL or CDSL.</p>
</section>
<p>It is needed for shares and ETFs and may be used for bonds. It is an ownership record, not a guarantee of investment quality.</p>
<p><strong>Protect ownership records.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Open demat with a regulated depository participant.</li>
  <li>Protect login, TPIN, and DIS instructions.</li>
  <li>Reconcile holdings periodically.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Broker</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> a low-cost broker may be enough for simple investing, while frequent traders need stronger tools and risk controls. Brokerage choice should reduce friction, not encourage unnecessary trades.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>A SEBI-registered broker provides market access for buying and selling Indian securities.</p>
</section>
<p>The broker is an intermediary, not the owner of your investment decision. Low brokerage is useful, but platform reliability and compliance also matter.</p>
<p><strong>Use regulated access.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Use a registered broker.</li>
  <li>Avoid margin and derivatives unless fully understood.</li>
  <li>Check contract notes and charges.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Nominee</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> adding a nominee to demat and mutual fund folios helps heirs access assets faster. A missing nominee can turn a simple portfolio into a documentation problem.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Nomination helps transfer access after death under Indian account processes.</p>
</section>
<p>Operational clarity is part of financial discipline. Nomination should be reviewed with broader legal planning.</p>
<p><strong>Keep nomination current.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Add nominee to all investment accounts.</li>
  <li>Review nomination after major life events.</li>
  <li>Keep legal heir and will considerations separate from simple nomination.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Password safety</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> a compromised email, phone, or broker login can expose financial accounts. Strong passwords, two-factor authentication, and separate email hygiene protect capital from operational risk.</p>
<p>Indian financial accounts need strong authentication. A weak password is an open gate to assets.</p>
<p><strong>Protect access.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Use unique strong passwords and two-factor authentication.</li>
  <li>Never share passwords, OTPs, or PINs.</li>
  <li>Use a trusted password manager if needed.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Fraud calls</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> calls promising guaranteed stock tips, IPO allotment, or KYC updating can push investors into sharing OTPs or installing remote-access apps. Genuine regulated institutions do not need such shortcuts.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Fraud calls exploit urgency, fear, greed, or authority to make you act before thinking.</p>
</section>
<p>No genuine institution needs your OTP or password over a call.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-law">
  <h4>Pause and verify</h4>
<ul>
  <li>Disconnect and contact official customer care yourself.</li>
  <li>Do not install remote-access apps on request.</li>
  <li>Treat guaranteed-return calls as danger signals.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Fake tips</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> social-media groups often show selective screenshots and hide losses. A tip without process, valuation, and risk control is entertainment wearing the costume of advice.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Fake tips promise certainty in uncertain markets and often profit from your attention, trades, or losses.</p>
</section>
<p>Prediction without accountability is noise.</p>
<p><strong>Do not outsource judgment to rumours.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Ignore tips from social media, groups, and unknown callers.</li>
  <li>Never buy only because someone says it will rise.</li>
  <li>Build process instead of following rumours.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">SEBI investor protection</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> SEBI registration, SCORES complaints, exchange grievance systems, and official disclosures give investors formal protection routes. They do not remove risk, but they reduce abuse and opacity.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>SEBI regulates Indian securities markets and provides investor education and complaint mechanisms.</p>
</section>
<p>Regulation reduces abuse but cannot remove market risk. Investor protection works best when investors use official channels and keep records.</p>
<p><strong>Use regulated entities.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Deal with SEBI-registered entities.</li>
  <li>Keep transaction proofs and communication records.</li>
  <li>Use official complaint systems when needed.</li>
</ul>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-event">
  <h4>1992 - Securities scam in India</h4>
<p>The 1992 securities scam exposed weaknesses in market infrastructure and settlement practices. It led to stronger attention on regulation, transparency, dematerialization, and investor protection.</p>
</section>

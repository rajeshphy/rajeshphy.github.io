 <h2 class="act-section-heading"><span>15</span>Stock Market Basics</h2>
<p class="act-essence-box investment-intro">The stock market chapter gives the minimum language needed before buying individual shares. It begins with ownership, then company size, revenue, profit, debt, valuation, dividends, and PE ratio.</p>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Share</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> buying one share of an Indian listed company means owning a tiny claim on that business. The share price moves daily, but the underlying claim is on future profits and assets.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>A share is a unit of ownership in a company listed on Indian exchanges or otherwise issued under Indian securities law.</p>
</section>
<p>It gives claim on residual value after all obligations are met. Its price changes because expectations about future cash flows change.</p>
<p><strong>Risk capital only.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Buy shares only with risk capital.</li>
  <li>Understand the business before buying individual stocks.</li>
  <li>Do not confuse price movement with business quality.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Company ownership</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> shareholders of a bank or consumer company are owners after lenders and employees are paid. Ownership can create wealth only if the company creates value over time.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Owning equity means participating in business outcomes.</p>
</section>
<p>If the company grows profitably, owners may benefit. If it fails, owners can lose. Equity holders are last in the claim hierarchy, so return potential is paired with risk.</p>
<p><strong>Think like an owner.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Think like an owner, not a ticket holder.</li>
  <li>Study earnings, debt, management, and industry.</li>
  <li>Diversify ownership.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Market capitalization</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> a company with 100 crore shares at INR 500 has a market capitalization of INR 50,000 crore. Market cap tells what the market currently values the equity at, not what the company earned last year.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Market capitalization equals share price multiplied by number of shares. It is the market's current INR value for the company.</p>
</section>
<p>Large, mid, and small caps differ in maturity, liquidity, growth potential, and risk.</p>
<p><strong>Size is context.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Use market cap to understand size and risk.</li>
  <li>Do not assume small means cheap or large means safe.</li>
  <li>Compare companies within relevant sectors.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Revenue</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> a retailer may report rising sales because it opened more stores, but revenue alone does not show whether the growth is profitable. It is the top line, not the final outcome.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Revenue is money earned from selling goods or services before expenses. It is the top line.</p>
</section>
<p>Revenue growth matters only if it can eventually become cash profit.</p>
<p><strong>Sales are not enough.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Check whether revenue is recurring, cyclical, or one-time.</li>
  <li>Compare revenue growth with margin and cash flow.</li>
  <li>Avoid valuing a company on sales alone.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Profit</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> a software company with high margins and low debt may convert revenue into profit better than a capital-heavy business. Profit quality matters because owners are paid from residual earnings.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Profit is what remains after costs, interest, depreciation, and tax.</p>
</section>
<p>Profit is an accounting measure of business surplus. Its quality depends on cash conversion, sustainability, and capital required to produce it.</p>
<p><strong>Check profit quality.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Study profit along with operating cash flow.</li>
  <li>Prefer durable profit over temporary spikes.</li>
  <li>Watch margin trends.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Debt</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> a company borrowing heavily for expansion may do well if cash flows arrive, but suffer if demand slows or rates rise. Debt magnifies both success and distress.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Company debt is borrowed money that must be serviced through interest and principal repayment.</p>
</section>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-theory">
  <h4>Leverage magnifies outcomes</h4>
<p>Debt can improve return when business is strong and destroy equity when cash flow weakens. Debt is a fixed obligation; equity return is variable.</p>
</section>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-law">
  <h4>Respect leverage</h4>
<ul>
  <li>Check debt-to-equity, interest coverage, and cash flow.</li>
  <li>Be cautious with high debt in cyclical businesses.</li>
  <li>Compare leverage with industry norms.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Valuation</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> a good Indian company bought at an extreme price can still produce poor investor returns. Valuation connects business quality with the price paid for that quality.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Valuation links INR price to business fundamentals.</p>
</section>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-theory">
  <h4>Discounted cash flow principle</h4>
<p>Value is related to future cash flows adjusted for risk and time. A good Indian company can be a poor investment if bought at an extreme price.</p>
</section>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-law">
  <h4>Price matters</h4>
<ul>
  <li>Compare price with earnings, cash flow, book value, and growth.</li>
  <li>Avoid buying only because the story is attractive.</li>
  <li>Demand margin of safety when uncertainty is high.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Dividend</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> mature companies may distribute part of profit as dividends, while growing companies may reinvest. For an Indian resident, dividends are cash flow but also taxable income.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Dividend is a distribution of profit to shareholders.</p>
</section>
<p>It is one form of owner return. A high dividend is useful only if the business can sustain it without damaging growth or balance sheet.</p>
<p><strong>Do not chase yield alone.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Check dividend payout and cash generation.</li>
  <li>Do not chase dividend yield without business quality.</li>
  <li>Remember dividends may be taxed.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">PE ratio</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> a PE of 40 means investors are paying forty rupees for one rupee of annual earnings. The ratio is meaningful only after considering growth, stability, cyclicality, and accounting quality.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Price-to-earnings ratio equals market price per share divided by earnings per share.</p>
</section>
<p>PE tells how much investors pay for one unit of earnings. It is meaningful only with growth, quality, cyclicality, and accounting context.</p>
<p><strong>Use PE with context.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Compare PE within similar businesses.</li>
  <li>Low PE can signal value or trouble.</li>
  <li>High PE requires strong and durable growth justification.</li>
</ul>

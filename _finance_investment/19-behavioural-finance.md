 <h2 class="act-section-heading"><span>19</span>Behavioural Finance</h2>
<p class="act-essence-box investment-intro">Behavioural finance studies how investors damage their own plans. This chapter follows the emotional sequence: greed, fear, panic selling, FOMO, herd mentality, overconfidence, and daily portfolio checking.</p>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Greed</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> in a rising small-cap market, investors may increase allocation just because neighbors made money. Greed replaces expected return with entitlement and usually ignores downside.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Greed is the desire for return without respect for risk.</p>
</section>
<p>It appears strongest after recent gains and pushes investors toward leverage, concentration, and products they do not understand.</p>
<p><strong>Respect downside.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Treat unusually high promised return as a risk signal.</li>
  <li>Keep allocation limits even in bull markets.</li>
  <li>Ask what can go wrong before investing.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Fear</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> after a sudden market fall, the same investor who wanted high returns may want to exit everything. Fear compresses time horizon exactly when patience is most valuable.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Fear is the emotional response to possible loss.</p>
</section>
<p>Fear is useful when it detects real danger and harmful when it forces irrational exit. Markets exploit unprepared fear through volatility.</p>
<p><strong>Prepare before decline.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Expect declines before they happen.</li>
  <li>Hold only the equity level you can survive.</li>
  <li>Do not sell long-term assets only because prices fell.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Panic selling</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> selling equity funds during a crash converts temporary price decline into permanent capital loss. Panic selling is usually a liquidity or temperament failure, not an investment strategy.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Panic selling is selling because price has fallen and emotion has taken control.</p>
</section>
<p>It converts temporary price decline into permanent loss. It usually happens when risk was misunderstood before investing.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-law">
  <h4>Use written exits</h4>
<ul>
  <li>Write exit rules before market stress.</li>
  <li>Keep near-term money out of equity.</li>
  <li>Review fundamentals and goal horizon before selling.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">FOMO</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> when IPOs, crypto-like themes, or hot sectors dominate conversation, investors may buy only to avoid feeling left out. FOMO makes someone else's return the basis for your risk.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Fear of missing out is the urge to enter after others have already made money.</p>
</section>
<p>It mistakes observed motion for future certainty. By the time a theme is socially obvious, risk may already be priced badly.</p>
<p><strong>Accept missed gains.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Do not buy because everyone is discussing it.</li>
  <li>Compare every new idea with your written allocation.</li>
  <li>Accept that missing some gains is part of discipline.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Herd mentality</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> if everyone in an office buys the same thematic fund after one strong year, the crowd may already have moved the price. Herding feels safe socially but can be dangerous financially.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Herd mentality replaces analysis with crowd movement.</p>
</section>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-theory">
  <h4>Feedback loop</h4>
<p>Markets are social systems with feedback loops. Imitation can amplify bubbles and crashes because each participant treats the previous participant's action as evidence.</p>
</section>
<p><strong>Demand independent reason.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Demand independent reason for every investment.</li>
  <li>Avoid crowded products without understanding valuation and risk.</li>
  <li>Use rules when crowd emotion is loud.</li>
</ul>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-event">
  <h4>1720 - South Sea Bubble</h4>
<p>The South Sea Bubble showed how stories, crowd excitement, and easy profit expectations can detach prices from reality. The event remains useful because investor psychology has changed less than market technology.</p>
</section>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Overconfidence</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> a few profitable trades can convince an investor that skill is higher than it is. Overconfidence increases position size before the process has been tested across bad markets.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Overconfidence is believing skill is higher than evidence supports.</p>
</section>
<p>It often follows lucky gains. In markets, a small sample can look like proof while being statistical noise.</p>
<p><strong>Use position limits.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Track decisions and outcomes honestly.</li>
  <li>Limit position size when uncertain.</li>
  <li>Prefer boring diversification unless you have real edge.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Checking portfolio daily</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> daily app checking makes long-term capital feel like a scoreboard. The more often the investor observes noise, the more likely they are to act on noise.</p>
<p>Daily checking increases emotional sampling. More observations create more chances to see loss, even when long-term probability is acceptable.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-theory">
  <h4>Wrong sampling frequency</h4>
<p>High-frequency monitoring of a low-frequency goal produces anxiety. A retirement goal does not need daily measurement.</p>
</section>
<p><strong>Reduce noise.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Review long-term portfolio monthly or quarterly, not daily.</li>
  <li>Check goal progress annually.</li>
  <li>Remove apps or alerts that trigger impulsive action.</li>
</ul>

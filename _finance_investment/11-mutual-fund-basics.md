 <h2 class="act-section-heading"><span>11</span>Mutual Fund Basics</h2>
<p class="act-essence-box investment-intro">Mutual funds pool investor money and invest it according to a stated scheme mandate. This chapter explains the basic measurement language first: NAV, units, AUM, cost, exit load, benchmark, fund manager, and direct versus regular plans.</p>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">NAV</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> two Nifty index funds can have different NAVs because they started at different dates, yet both may hold similar portfolios. The lower NAV is not cheaper; the percentage movement matters.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Net Asset Value is the per-unit value of a mutual fund:</p>
\[
\text{NAV}=\frac{\text{assets}-\text{liabilities}}{\text{units outstanding}}.
\]
</section>
<p>NAV is not expensive or cheap by itself. A fund at NAV 500 is not necessarily costlier than one at NAV 20.</p>
<p><strong>Do not rank by NAV.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Judge funds by portfolio, strategy, risk, cost, and performance.</li>
  <li>Do not choose funds because NAV is low.</li>
  <li>NAV changes reflect underlying asset movement.</li>
</ul>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-event">
  <h4>1996 - SEBI mutual fund regulations</h4>
<p>SEBI's mutual fund regulatory framework gave Indian mutual funds a clearer structure for scheme operation, disclosure, trusteeship, and investor protection. The modern Indian mutual fund industry should be read through that regulated structure.</p>
</section>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Units</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> investing INR 10,000 at NAV 50 gives 200 units, while investing at NAV 100 gives 100 units. If both rise 10 per cent, wealth rises equally; unit count alone says nothing.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Units represent your share in a mutual fund scheme. Holding value equals units held multiplied by NAV.</p>
</section>
<p>When you invest, you receive units according to the applicable NAV. When you redeem, units are reduced.</p>
<p><strong>Track value, not unit count.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Track units and NAV separately.</li>
  <li>More units do not imply more wealth unless value is higher.</li>
  <li>Redemptions reduce units.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">AUM</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> a tiny index fund may struggle with tracking and liquidity, while a very large small-cap fund may struggle to deploy money efficiently. AUM is context for execution, not a medal.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Assets Under Management is the total money managed by an Indian mutual fund scheme.</p>
</section>
<p>AUM indicates scale, but not automatically quality. Very small AUM may create viability or liquidity concerns; very large AUM may reduce flexibility in some strategies.</p>
<p><strong>Use AUM as context.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Use AUM as a context variable, not a ranking score.</li>
  <li>Be cautious with very small funds.</li>
  <li>For index funds, sufficient AUM helps efficient tracking.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Expense ratio</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> two similar index funds with different expense ratios will not deliver the same investor return over decades. The lower-cost fund starts each year with a small but certain advantage.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Expense ratio is the annual cost charged by the mutual fund scheme.</p>
</section>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-theory">
  <h4>Cost is certain</h4>
<p>Every rupee of cost directly reduces investor return. Excess performance is uncertain; cost is certain.</p>
</section>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-law">
  <h4>Control cost</h4>
<ul>
  <li>Prefer lower cost for similar strategy and quality.</li>
  <li>Direct plans usually have lower expense ratio than regular plans.</li>
  <li>Compare expense within the same fund category.</li>
</ul>
</section>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Exit load</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> an equity fund may charge an exit load if redeemed within a year. An investor using it for emergency money may discover that liquidity exists but has a cost.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Exit load is a charge for redeeming from an Indian mutual fund before a specified period.</p>
</section>
<p>It discourages short-term movement and matters when liquidity or near-term use is possible.</p>
<p><strong>Check exit before entry.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Check exit load before investing.</li>
  <li>Do not put emergency money in products with inconvenient exit rules.</li>
  <li>Include exit load in return calculations for early redemption.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Benchmark</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> a large-cap fund should not be celebrated merely for beating a savings account; it should be compared with Nifty 100 or the stated benchmark. The reference frame decides whether performance is meaningful.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>A benchmark is the reference index used to judge fund performance, such as Nifty 50, Nifty 500, Sensex, or a debt index.</p>
</section>
<p>Without a benchmark, performance has no reference frame. A fund should be compared with the index that matches its mandate.</p>
<p><strong>Use correct reference frame.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Compare a fund with the correct benchmark.</li>
  <li>Long-term underperformance after cost needs attention.</li>
  <li>Benchmark risk may differ from fund risk if the fund deviates strongly.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Fund manager</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> a star manager can have weak years even with a sound process, and a lucky year can flatter a weak process. Investors should ask whether decisions are repeatable and within mandate.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>The fund manager chooses securities according to the scheme mandate.</p>
</section>
<p>In active funds, manager skill and process matter. Even skilled managers can underperform for long periods because markets are noisy.</p>
<p><strong>Judge process, not fame.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Evaluate process, consistency, and risk control.</li>
  <li>Do not chase a manager after one good year.</li>
  <li>Prefer simple passive funds if manager selection is difficult.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="investment-section-heading">Direct vs regular plan</h3>
<p class="investment-section-story"><strong>Example:</strong> the same scheme can have a direct plan and a regular plan with different costs. A do-it-yourself investor may keep the cost saving, while an advised investor must judge whether advice earns its fee.</p>
<section class="investment-box investment-box-definition">
<p>Direct plans are bought without distributor commission and usually have lower expense ratio. Regular plans include distributor commission and may include advice service.</p>
</section>
<p>The economic difference compounds over time. Advice has value only when it improves decisions by more than its cost.</p>
<p><strong>Pay only for useful advice.</strong></p>
<ul>
  <li>Use direct plans if you can choose and manage funds yourself.</li>
  <li>Use regular plans only when advice quality justifies the cost.</li>
  <li>Compare the same scheme's direct and regular expense ratios.</li>
</ul>
